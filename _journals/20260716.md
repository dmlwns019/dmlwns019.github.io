# *What is Toastboard?*
Toastboard is a modular microcontroller-controlled electric circuit base kit designed for students to learn electrical concepts intuitively, rather than simply memorizing facts. A Toastboard kit contains three categories of parts necessary to operate: the “toaster” (microcontroller), the “crumb” (breadboard module), and the “jam” (components). Toastboard is aimed at creating an accessible way for primary to middle-school students to learn electrical engineering concepts intuitively, providing a bridge for more advanced systems such as the Arduino. Using lights and visual feedback, the goal of Toastboard is to provide a physical engineering education experience where hardware gives the same visual and intuitive feedback a visual programming language might.
<img src="/journalAssets/20260716/toastCadVisualization.png" alt="Toastboard Image" width="650">

Toastboard is primarily designed for the ease-of-use of curious primary and middle-school students, approximately ages 8-14. Toastboard is also aimed towards self-taught individuals without access to early formal engineering courses and education. However, Toastboard is not solely a toy for children, but an intuitive bridge. Beginners in electrical engineering of all ages should be able to use the product, but Toastboard is not actively designed for more complex levels of education.


## Short answer: Toastboard is an engineering kit finding an answer to: what if learning electrical engineering was as easy as connecting pieces, and as intuitive as building legos?”



# *Okay.. but what's the issue with current engineering education?*

Of course, Toastboard isn't claiming to be a revolutionary piece in engineering education, nor is it being created to replace any current standard engineering systems. Toastboard is not meant to replace an Arduino or Breadboard, but rather to provide a solid foundation so that younger engineering students can visualize abstract engineering concepts through visual and intuitive feedback. 

Toastboard responds to three large issues identified with many current engineering education kits, including:
### 1. The product is designed for entertainment, not education
### 2. The product encourages students follow step-by-step instructions rather than intuitive learning
### 3. The product is rigid in functionality, education, and possible project range

**However, it's important to note that Toastboard isn’t meant to replace electronics education entirely. Rather, it's a different perspective on platforms designed for students experimenting with electronics, and as a possible tool used alongside other education systems in order to deepen and broaden insight on how engineering concepts can be intuitively understood and applied practically. ** 

# *What are Toastboard's features?*

Here are some of Toastboard's key features:

### 1. Modular design -- Allows for cost-effectiveness, easy repairs, and flexibility in innovation and experimentation
### 2. Intuitive connections -- Gives visual and intuitive feedback, allowing for obvious yet intentional relationships
### 3. Real-world engineering concepts that are honest, yet simplified -- The system is set up in a way where students, when transitioning from Toastboard to more complex engineering systems, can recognize and innovate with the same components used

# *What are Toastboard's design principles?*
### 1. Innovation over instruction -- Toastboard doesn’t promote rigid instructions on what students should create, but rather encourages individual learning that can be applied practically and personally 
### 2. Honest simplicity -- Toastboard should be open on how internal processes and functions work together to create systems without being hidden with black box abstractions
### 3. Encouraged experimentation -- Toastboard is designed not to eliminate mistakes, but rather to help students understand intuitively why these mistakes happen
### 4. Visible comprehension -- Toastboard should make the invisible, abstract concepts in hardware engineering visible and intuitive
### 5. Affordability over complexity -- Because of Toastboard's demographic of younger self-taught students, a lower cost outcome should be held to a higher standard than a powerful, feature-rich product


## *Alright, so now that our project's concept is out of the way, what are the realistic aspects of it?*

# *Budget*

Because Toastboard is an independent passion project, it is quite difficult to gain funding. Although grants and sponsors may be plausible options in the future, the early prototype and design stages will likely require personal investment. A budget of roughly $200 should be enough, estimating:
3D print materials – $40
Microcontrollers - $50
Components - $40
General electrical parts (wires, clips, etc.) - $30
Extra funds - $40

# *Scope of first prototype*

Toastboard’s initial prototype should not be a fully fleshed-out design, but rather a simple physical module system exploring whether physical modular electronics can provide a more intuitive and creative introduction to engineering. The prototype will focus on creating a small set of modules allowing beginners to create systems without traditional wiring. A list of basic features for the first prototype could include:

- 3-5 crumb modules
	- Basic enclosure
	- Simple design
	- Main focus is on how each node can have individual lighting
- Simple components
	- Input modules:
	- Button
	- Potentiometer
- Output modules:
	- LED
	- Servo motor
- Arduino-based brain
	- Programming will be controlled with regular Arduino API
